Ghost hoaxes and media in 19th century London

About the project

My intention with this project was to gain an understanding of Victorian media culture through the study of ghost hoaxes. In the collection of newspaper and periodical clippings, I have found examples of media attitudes that have confirmed and complicated my previous ideas about the attitudes and beliefs of reporters and journalists in nineteenth century London.

This website itself aims to provide context for the collection, as the emergent media was a complex and ever-changing system. As Aled Jones notes in Powers of the Press, “[t]he technology of speed in composing, printing and distribution, allowed print to acquire a teleology, a historical trajectory that, as the… editor of the Monthly Repository, W.J. Fox (1786-1864) wistfully explained, began with priests, extended to men of letters and ‘so from on grade of society to another the influence of the press passed, and spread wider and wider, till the vast reading public arose, and the reign of patronage passed away’”(Jones 74). Cultural fixation on the supernatural accompanied a swift transfusion of media power into the middle and lower classes. Such a rush of information sharing led to disparate ideas of what media should look like. Class, profession, gender, location, and politics played into who read what when. In the spirit of taking an intersectional view of these hoax stories, I attempt to explore misinformation within its moment. By virtue of that intersectionality, some pieces of this site may seem disparate, but are intended to give the user an understanding around this cultural event, rather than an understanding of it.
