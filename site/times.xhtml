Spiritualism vs. Science: The Times as forum and folly

In late 1872, The Times published a series of Letters to the Editor on the subject of spiritualism and science—and whether spiritualism deserved to be studied with any vigor. In their final editorial on the subject, they asserted that, of course these “popular delusions,” though “absurd,” were “too important to be neglected by the wiser portion of mankind” (Many sensible). In the spirit of public debate, however, they had printed a series which included reports of séances, sightings, and Spiritualist theory. I have collected the series here, along with the response of The Spiritualist, a weekly publication devoted to the study and promotion of Spiritualism.

This back and forth strikes me as an interesting moment for Victorian media. We have the well-regarded Times, a daily paper, challenged by a small weekly intended for a niche audience. The general attitude of the press regarding their own public influence was complex. Aled Jones’ Powers the Press: Newspapers, Power and the Public in Nineteenth-Century England states that “[j]ournalists were not isolated from trends in intellectual and popular culture,” referring to mesmerism, spiritualism, and paranormal psychology (Jones 79). W.T. Stead, editor of The Pall Mall Gazette, was a noted spiritualist, even publishing Real Ghost Stories later in his life. The press had no singular attitude toward spiritualism, and so had no singular influence on popular opinion of the topic.

Though a singular attitude could not be imparted, papers were able to offer news, “narrativized and infused with an ‘attitude of suspense,’ particularly through reprintings (Jones 84). In the collection offered on the maps page, I include a few reprints from the counties as examples of these stories. After 1855’s repeal of the Stamp Tax, viral and suspenseful material was cheap to print and reprint, and it sold. Early penny dreadfuls (even before the Stamp Act) were seen by larger newspapers and the Anglican church as possibly harmful content for lower class people, but by 1859 historian Charles Macintosh observed “that, within a comparatively recent period, an observable shift had occurred in the perceived social value of popular newspapers” (Jones 109). This shift correlates with an observed decline in the publication of ‘immoral fiction,’ and uptick in ‘wholesome fiction,’ the definitions of which are based mainly on the presence of violence or mystery in the story (Jones 111). Ghost hoaxes, which often included attacks and suspenseful language, would have remained an immoral genre.

The series in The Times represents a fixation on the immoral, misguided, and foolish beliefs in society, and yet papers continued to publish and people continued to read. The judgement imparted by The Times editorials along with The Spiritualist’s verbose defense represent a cultural push and pull fueled by a complex and ever-changing media.
